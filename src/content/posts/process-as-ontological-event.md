To make art is to participate in what Etienne Souriau, as interpreted by Bruno Latour, called *instauration.* Not the actualization of a pre-existing ideal, but a genuinely risky invention in which both artist and work co-emerge through the doing.

This framework resists the tired binary of form and matter, of artist as sovereign and material as passive medium. The work is not discovered so much as it is coaxed into being through a process of mutual alteration, what Latour frames as *trying alteration as a mode of subsistence* rather than always searching for the substance beneath the alterations.

## Potentiality Within the Act

This ontological understanding of process finds its deepest philosophical grounding in Agamben's reading of Aristotle's distinction between potentiality and actuality. The masterful act of creation is not the one that simply converts potentiality into the finished object, but the one that, as Agamben insists, preserves potentiality within the act. It maintains the *power not to* alongside the *power to.*

> Mastery is not formal perfection but the salvation of imperfection in a perfect form.

The trembling hand of the artist, as Dante intuited, is not a defect but the very sign of authentic creative tension.

## The Ghost in the Fragment

Translated into the context of a process-driven art practice, one involving negation, deconstruction, and cyclical reformation of the same materials, this means that every act of destruction retains within it the latent potentiality of what is not yet made. The shredded print, the laser-cut fragment, the re-etched matrix: each carries within it the ghostly presence of what it was and the as yet unactualized form of what it might become.
