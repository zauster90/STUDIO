Walter Benjamin's diagnosis of the withering of the aura under conditions of mechanical reproduction remains one of the most generative provocations in modern aesthetics. Not because it is simply correct but because the problem it names has only deepened.

As Agamben reads Benjamin, technical reproducibility carries authenticity to an extreme rather than simply destroying it: the multiplication of the original turns authenticity into the very cipher of elusiveness, something more desired the less it can be located.

## The Vehicle of the Intransmissible

Baudelaire's response to this condition, as Agamben frames it, was to *reproduce within the work itself the very destruction of transmissibility.* To make the work into a vehicle of the intransmissible rather than a container of transmitted meaning.

> Art survives the ruin of tradition not by recuperating lost authority but by thematizing its own inability to transmit.

This is a radical aesthetic position: making the gap between past and present its subject matter rather than its problem.

## The Wound, Not the Resource

A practice built on cycles of making, documentation, destruction, and reformation participates in exactly this logic. The laser-cut fragments of a finished print, reorganized and woven into a new substrate, are not nostalgic for the original. They carry the memory of their former coherence as a wound rather than a resource, as something structurally marked by loss.

The resulting object does not communicate a content so much as it performs the condition of contemporary making: wedged between a past that accumulates faster than it can be read and a future that throws no light backward, the work holds that impossible position and refuses to look away from it.
