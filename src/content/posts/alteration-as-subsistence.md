---
title: "Alteration as Subsistence: Latour, Nancy, and the Art of Passage"
date: 2026-07-15
tags: [philosophy, ontology]
---
There is a question that sits underneath most serious thinking about artmaking and refuses to go away: what exactly is the ontological status of the thing being made? Not its cultural value, not its meaning, not its relationship to tradition or market or institution, but the more basic question of what kind of existence it has and how that existence is produced.

Bruno Latour, in his reading of the French philosopher Etienne Souriau, offers one of the most generative answers available. Souriau's concept of *instauration* resists the familiar story in which an artist holds an ideal form in mind and progressively actualizes it through the medium of matter. That story preserves a clean division between the sovereign subject and the passive material, the idea and its execution. Instauration refuses this division entirely.

The work to be made is not a destination the artist moves toward but a genuinely risky co-invention, a double modality in which the work and the artist discover themselves simultaneously in the laboring process. Souriau describes the moment of recognition that arrives at the end of this process, the sudden sense of inevitability that is only available in retrospect, as the clearest sign that something genuinely new has come into being rather than merely been transferred from mind to matter. What fascinates Latour about this account is precisely its irreducibility to either idealism or materialism. The work does not pre-exist its making. Neither does the maker, in any fully formed sense. Both emerge through the encounter.

Latour sharpens this into a broader ontological principle when he proposes, following Souriau, that we *try alteration as a mode of subsistence* rather than always searching for the substance lying beneath the alterations. This is a radical reversal of the usual philosophical priority. Western metaphysics has tended to assume that beneath the changing surface of things there lies a stable substrate, a substance that endures through alteration and which alteration merely modifies. Souriau and Latour invert this. There is no being beneath the alterations. The alteration is the being. If no alteration, then no being.

This is what Souriau designates as *being qua another:* existence understood not as persistence of the same but as a continual passage through difference, a subsistence that is constituted entirely by its own ongoing transformation. Applied to an art practice built on processes of negation, deconstruction, and material reformation, this ontology does not merely describe what is happening. It names it as the fundamental condition of existence itself. The shredded print that becomes a woven substrate is not degrading from one state to another. It is, in Souriau's sense, becoming more fully real through its alteration, finding its mode of subsistence in the passage rather than in any stable configuration it passes through.

Jean-Luc Nancy, approaching from a different direction through his reading of Hegel, arrives at a remarkably convergent position. In *The Restlessness of the Negative,* Nancy locates the philosophical center of Hegelian thought not in the dialectic as a mechanism for resolving contradiction into synthesis, but in what he calls the infinite restlessness of the present. Sense, for Nancy, is not a content that can be fixed and transmitted. It is what makes itself sensed and gives itself form in passage and as passage. It is nonfixity itself.

This means that the present moment of an artwork, the moment of its encounter with a viewer, with a material, with the next decision in the studio, is never a stable point of arrival but always a point of transition, what Nancy calls *a passage of thought through the point itself.* The point is the passage. And crucially, in this passage the one finds its truth in the other, is transformed by the encounter, unsettled in its own ground.

This is not a description of instability as failure. It is a description of how sense operates, how truth happens, not through fixity but through the restlessness that inhabits every present moment and refuses to let it calcify into mere facticity. What is asked of thought, Nancy writes, is precisely this: to not give up on the inscription of the absolute in the present, without ever absolutizing any present.

The artwork that refuses completion, that returns to its own materials, that remakes what it has made, is not exhibiting a pathology of incompletion. It is enacting the fundamental structure of sense itself as Nancy understands it: always in transit, always in relation, always already becoming something other than what it was, which is the only way it was ever truly itself.
