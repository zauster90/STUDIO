A persistent philosophical problem for the practicing artist is the inadequacy of language to communicate what the artwork does. This is not merely a practical frustration but an ontological condition.

As Alain Badiou observed, poetry cannot name its own consistency. There is no metalanguage adequate to what language itself can perform, and therefore only the absence of a definitive name permits the perpetual process of self-nomination.

## The Object That Withdraws

This insight finds an unexpected parallel in Object-Oriented Ontology's insistence that all objects, including artworks, perpetually withdraw from full disclosure. Harman's distinction between sensual and real objects, the former present to perception, the latter irreducibly concealed, means that the artwork always harbors an excess beyond what any encounter with it can exhaust.

> The human observer is a necessary ingredient of the aesthetic experience, but the artwork simultaneously exceeds and eludes that observer.

The artwork possesses real qualities that resist reduction to their perceptual appearance. This double structure, presence and withdrawal, disclosure and concealment, maps onto the ontological architecture of quantum nonlocality as described by Morton: two entangled particles, separated by impossible distances, communicating in ways that exceed any local account.

## Beyond Legibility

The artwork operates similarly as a kind of nonlocal event, its resonances extending beyond the physical conditions of any single encounter. What follows for practice is that the artist's ambition should not be the production of fully legible objects, but the cultivation of irreducible material presences. Things that manifest sense without collapsing it into signification. Things that speak, as Agamben writes, *in the name of a name that is missing.*
